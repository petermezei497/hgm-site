This series is not economic history.
It’s an observation of how systems treat people.

⸻

1) What did it respond to?

Feudalism responded to chaos.
To post-Roman instability,
the lack of a monopoly on violence,
and unpredictable protection.

It promised order where there had been none.

⸻

2) What did it reward?

Loyalty.
Role adherence.
Staying in place.

If you knew your position
and didn’t question too much,
you gained safety.

⸻

3) What did it punish?

Mobility.
Deviation.
Independence.

The system was stable —
as long as no one tried to leave it.

⸻

4) How did it handle risk?

In a split way.

Political and military risk sat at the top
(war, power struggles),
whilst everyday life risk remained at the bottom
(crops, hunger, disease).

In return, there was a promise:
protection.

⸻

5) What happened to money when it “worked”?

Money had limited weight.
Barter and in-kind exchange were common.
Money did not organise life around itself.

⸻

6) What was its corrective effect — and for how long?

It stabilised.
It created order.
It was more liveable than chaos.

But over time,
it froze movement
and became humanly expensive in its rigidity.

⸻

Early warning signals

1. Hardening:
When protection becomes control,
and stability demands immobility.

2. Risk-shifting:
When protection becomes conditional,
and life risk shifts one-sidedly downward.

3. Money distortion:
(This didn’t yet occur in feudalism)

⸻

How people lived in the system

…when it worked (Flow):
Predictability. Community. Role.
If you accepted your limits, there was peace.

Order instead of chaos.
Protection instead of uncertainty.

For roughly 200–300 years, it was liveable —
if you knew your place.

…when it began to harden (Friction):
No escape. No change. No agency.

If you didn’t fit, it was unliveable.

Protection became control.
Stability demanded immobility.

…overall:
Initially acceptable (vs. chaos).
Eventually unsustainable (vs. freedom).

Safety came at the cost of room to move.
What began as framework became prison.

More liveable than chaos.
Not liveable enough to last.


⸻

We don’t have to be quicker.
We don’t have to be tougher.
We need to be freer. ❤️
